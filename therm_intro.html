  • The industrial revolution of the 18th and 19th centuries prompted the need to understand the operation of the engines (primarily steam powered) being developed to drive that revolution.  The belief at the time was that heat was a form of energy distinct from kinetic and potential energies and that the operation of a steam engine somehow converted heat energy into mechanical (kinetic and potential) energy.  With the development of the atomic physics and quantum mechanics in the last century we now know that heat is not a distinct form of energy, but can be thought of as the mechanical energy of the atoms and molecules of which matter is comprised.
  • The theory of heat developed in the 19th century involved the macroscopic properties of materials (pressure, volume, temperature etc.) and was called THERMODYNAMICS.  It was very successful in speeding engine development and is essential in today's society, where we obtain almost all of our energy via some sort of combustion of fossil fuels in which heat is converted to mechanical work (energy).
  • As indcated above Thermodynamics assumes no knowledge of the detailed composition of materials.  Nevertheless, with a knowledge of the microscopic properties of materials (speeds, masses, momenta, energies of the constituent atoms and molecules), it is possible to create a theory of heat which uses this information and produces the same results.  This theory is called STATISTICAL MECHANICS.
  • Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics are two distinct theories for predicting the properites of systems where heat energy is important.  Whichever theory is applied the results obtained will be the same.
  • The time constraints of this course will limit our discussion primarily to Thermodynamics.  However, we may have cause to refer to some of the results of statistical mechanics applied to gases known as KINETIC THEORY.
