  • The concept of temperature is central to any discussion of thermodynamics, but what is temperature ?  Can we define it precisely ? 
  • Of course the answer is yes.  In fact the precise definition was deemed so important that its definition is based on the "zeroth" law of thermodynamics (called the zeroth law since its statement is essential to the other laws of thermodynamics, but was not formally designated a law until after the first and second laws had been accepted).
  • Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics
"If two systems A and C are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, B, then A and C are in thermal equilibrium with each other.  When two systems are in thermal equilibrium they have the same temperature."

Furthermore, when two systems are placed in contact heat (energy) will flow from one to the other until they are in thermal equilibrium with each other at which time thay have the same temperature.


  • Measurement of Temperature and Temperature Scales
In order to define a temperature scale we need a physical quantity which varies with temperature, for example,
  • Length of a metal rod
  • Volume of a liquid
  • Electrical resistance
  • Pressure of a gas kept at constant volume
The last property may seem rather obscure and impractical for a thermometer.  However, this type of thermometer has the essential property that it can be made independent of the gas used.  That is it provides a reproduceable "standard" temperature scale.

  • Constant volume gas thermometer
Using the configuration shown at right a temperature scale can be defined using the following steps
  1. Define the temperature as T = aP, where a is a constant (we know that P is proportional to T from the ideal gas law PV=nRT).
  2. Choose a "standard" fixed point - T = 273.16 at the triple point of water.  The triple point of water is a unique temperature at which solid, liquid and gaseous water can co-exist and is reproduceable.  Then we have  a = 273.16/PTr , where PTr is the pressure of the gas at the triple point.
  3. Therefore, T = 273.16 (P/PTr)
  4. But experimentally it is found that the temperature of, say, boiling water depends very slightly on the type of gas used.  However, if we gradually decrease the amount of gas, i.e. decrease PTr for each gas then the temperature measured by each gas approaches a single value (373).
  5. As PTr -> 0 we have the "ideal gas" scale, which is the standard against which all other thermometers are calibrated.
  • Note that in the above scheme we have defined the Kelvin or Absolute temperature scale.  On this scale the temperature at which the pressure is zero is called absolute zero, see below,



  • The Celsius (or centigrade) scale has the same degree size as the Kelvin scale, but the triple point temperature is defined as 0.01 oC.  In other words the scale is simply shifted with respect to the Kelvin scale.
  • The Fahrenheit and Rankine temperature scales have a smaller degree size, such that
TF = 9TC/5 + 32

TR = TF + 460

  • A few words about absolute zero. 
    • The Kelvin temperature at which the pressure is zero is defined to be zero because it is not possible for a gas to have a negative pressure.  This implies that temperatures below absolute zero (negative temperatures) cannot be achieved. 
    • However, a statistical mechanical description of temperature together with quantum mechanics, goes one step further, prediciting that absolute zero itself is not attainable.
    • Using Statistical Mechanics it can be shown that the temperature of a system directly proportional to the kinetic energy contained in the system.  As the temperature is reduced the kinetic energy decreases, so that when absolute zero is reached the kinetic energy is zero - all the atoms and molecules of the system are at rest.
    • But quantum mechanics, through the Uncertainty Principle, does not allow atomic-sized particles to be at rest.  No matter how cool the system its atoms will always have a "zero point energy", meaning that the system can never quite achieve absolute zero.
    • This prediction, which has been extensively verified by experiment, is a result of the third law of thermodynamics.
