Buoyancy and Archimedes Principle



"Eureka !"
("I've got it !")
Archimedes

 
  • If an object is  submerged (or partially submerged) in a fluid the buoyant force is the force which tends to push the object towards the surface.

    • It is this force which allows boats to float, balloons to rise and causes objects to weigh less when underwater.
     
  • Archimedes Principle                                  


    • " The buoyant force on a submerged or partially submerged object is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object "
     

  • The magnitude of the buoyant force determines whether an object will sink or float.


    • If

    weight of object  >  buoyant force   =>  object sinks  and its density is greater than the fluid

    weight of object  <  buoyant force   =>  object floats and its density is less than the fluid

  • Interesting example of buoyancy.  A can of diet coke floats in water, a can of classic coke does not float - demonstration.
  • Icebergs are frozen blocks of freshwater "calved" from glaciers or formed during the break-up of Winter pack-ice.  Icebergs float in the ocean because the density of freshwater is less than that of saltwater.  However, since there is only a small difference between these densities icebergs float with most (about 90%) of their mass underwater.  This can lead to disasterous results as in the case of  the RMS Titanic .   This picture demonstrates that "what you see is not necessarily what you get...".  But is this a "real" photograph ?  Click here for an explanation.

  • Problems involving buoyancy and Archimedes principle are often most easily solved by considering the equilibrium or otherwise of the forces acting on the object in question.
