  • The first law of thermodynamics is no more than a statement of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems.

  • As we have indicated above, although the first law can be applied to any thermodynamic process on a system, for this course we will limit our applications to systems of gases.  There are several important processes which can be defined by the particular behaviour of one (or more) of the thermodynamic variables.
  • Isochoric process - Takes place at constant V (volume), = 0, so that W (work done by the gas)  = 0 or 
  • Isobaric process - Takes place at constant p (pressure)
  • Isothermal process - Takes place at constant T (temperature)

For gases it can be shown via statistical mechanics, that the internal energy of the system is proportional to the temperature.  Thus, if we keep T constant (isothermal process) then U is also constant, = 0 = Q - W.  Or  Q = W.

  • Adbiabatic process - Takes place in such a way that Q = 0 so that  = -W = -pdV.  In order to achieve this we isolate the system from its surroundings or ensure that the process takes place very quickly.  Heat takes time to flow, if the process is performed quickly enough, it is a good approximation that no heat enters or leaves.
The internal combustion engine and the steam engine involve cyclic use of adiabatic expansion and compression of gases to achieve mechanical power output.  More about heat engines when we discuss the second law of thermodynamics.

If a gas expands rapidly, then it does work and W > 0.  This means that  = Uf - Ui < 0, or Uf < Ui so that Tf < Ti.  In other words an adiabatic expansion will decrease the temperature of a gas.  This is one of the easiest mechanisms to achieve ultra-low temperatures.  Note that, the reverse process is taking place when you use a bicycle pump, the temperature of the gas increases.
