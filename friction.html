  • Origin of friction is twofold
    1. Mechanical interlocking of "rough" surfaces
    2. Microscopic level - atomic attraction

Can be reduced by lubricants, e.g. oil grease.

  • All frictional forces act to impede motion.


Mass (inertia) does not impede motion.  If there is no friction, even a very small force will accelerate a very massive body.

  • Force needed to maintain motion is less than that required to start it.  Kinetic versus static frictional force.

  • Frictional Force Law

 

    • N - Normal force.  This is the perpendicular force between the two objects.


The normal force is not necessarily the weight of the object.

    • µ - coefficient of friction.
      • Determined by the types of material in contact.
      • For given surfaces µ is constant over a large range of speeds and independent of contact area.
      • µs  - coefficient of static friction, relevant when objects are at rest relative to each other

 

 

      • µk  - coefficient of kinetic friction, applicable when objects are in relative motion

 




  • Example problem

“An experimental physicist performs an experiment involving two cats, and an inclined tin roof.
The two cats are very nearly identical; same sex, age, weight, breed, eye and hair color.
The physicist places both cats on the roof at the same height and lets them both go at the same time. One of the cats falls off the roof first so obviously there is some difference between the two cats.
What is the difference?
One cat has a greater mew.”
